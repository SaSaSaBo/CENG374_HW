Bridges

KİLEZDERE KÖPRÜSÜ: The bridge, dating back to the 16th century, is over Başiskele-Kilezdere. In 1998, for the purpose of the "Izmit Urban Four-Line Railway Construction", it was dismantled from its original place as a result of the decision of the Preservation Board and rebuilt 50 meters away.

KUTLUCA KÖPRÜSÜ: The 25-meter-long historical Roman Bridge, also known as Taşköprü or Kutluca Bridge, was built from smooth cut block stones. German Karl Dörner, who conducted research in Kocaeli in 1940, determined that the building was built during the reign of Emperor Claudius by looking at its architectural features. The masonry and arch technique of the 7-arched and 5-arched bridge, dating back to the first century AD, are remarkable.

SERVETİYE TAŞ KÖPRÜ: It is in Servetiye village, on Kirazdere, which carries water to Yuvacık Dam. Both its architectural features and the stories told by local residents indicate that the bridge may have been built by masters migrating from the Black Sea in the 19th century. Although there is no more information about the bridge since its inscription has not survived to the present day, it is a work worth seeing with its sparkling stream flowing under it and the lush green nature around it.

SULTAN SÜLEYMAN KÖPRÜSÜ – MİMAR SİNAN KÖPRÜSÜ: It was built by Mimar Sinan in the early 16th century. It is on Diliskelesi, east of Gebze. The fact that the bridge has survived to the present day without any maintenance or repair reveals how solidly it was built.

VALİDE SULTAN KÖPRÜSÜ: Valide Kösem Sultan, one of the most famous ladies in Ottoman history, was the wife of Ahmet I and IV. She is Murat's mother. Valide Kösem Sultan had many mosques, masjids, bridges, inns, fountains and waterways built in different regions of the Ottoman geography, and left charitable contributions by establishing various foundations. One of these is the Valide Sultan Bridge in Kocaeli. Valide Sultan Bridge, which gave its name to the district where it was founded, is one of the most important works in the city from the Ottoman Period. It is on the road that starts from Karamürsel, passes through the villages of Karaahmetli, Hayriye, Yalakdere and goes to Iznik. It was built with cut stones in the 17th century, in the style of Classical Turkish Architecture.

Mansions

DEMİRCİLER KONAĞI: The mansion located in Dilovası Demirciler village is the most successful example of 19th century Ottoman Architecture. It is the only example in Kocaeli in terms of its hand-carved decorations and architectural style.

KAISER II. WILHELM KÖŞKÜ: In order to host foreign visitors coming to Hereke, it was decided to build a suitable mansion in honor of the state and the factory. Before the visit of German Emperor Kaiser Wilhelm II to Izmit, a feverish work begins at Yıldız Palace, by the order of Sultan Abdulhamid II. The mansion, which was built in Istanbul in Yıldız Palace in three weeks, was brought in pieces by sea and assembled in one day. In the construction of the mansion, which is thought to have been built by Italian Architect Raimondo D'Aronco, boat construction technique was applied and no metal nails were used. The mansion, which was built entirely of wooden construction, has many doors opening to both the sea and land sides, as an interesting design feature. Hereke-woven carpets, silk upholstery and draperies were used to furnish the mansion.

PEMBE KÖŞK: The Pink Mansion, located in Izmit-Upper Pazar, was taken over from a non-Muslim family to the treasury. The building, which has 19th century architectural features, consists of three floors above the basement and is made of Baghdadi plaster on wood. The second floor of the mansion, which was built in the form of guillotine windows, protrudes outside and is supported by supports. It was named the Pink Pavilion because of its beautiful color that attracts attention from those who see it.

PORTAKAL HAFIZ KONAĞI: The mansion, which once belonged to Hafız Rüştü Efendi, who joined the Ottoman Parliament as an Izmit Deputy, became known as the "Portakal Hafız Mansion" when the owner was given the nickname Portakal Hafız by the public due to the roundness of his face. The mansion, which shows the characteristics of 19th century Turkish residential architecture, has a three-hall plan arrangement. Rectangular shaped windows reflect the traditional features of Izmit houses. One of its most striking aspects is its wide eaves.

SAATÇİ ALİ EFENDİ KONAĞI – GÜMÜŞLÜOĞLU KONAĞI: The Old Ethnography Museum, also known as the "Saatçi Ali Efendi Mansion", was built in 1776. The building, on a sloping slope overlooking the sea on Alaca Mescit Hill in Veli Ahmet District, is one of the earliest mansions of Izmit that has survived to the present day. It has historical and cultural importance in terms of reflecting the concepts of taste and family character of Turkish architecture. As architectural elements, windows with stained glass, double glazing, arches, wooden shutters and sockets, and bars attract attention. The mansion is one of the examples of civil architecture that best reflects its period with its hand-carved decorations on its exterior and interior walls.

SIRRI PAŞA KONAĞI: The plan of the mansion built by Izmit Governor Selim Sırrı Pasha in the 19th century was also drawn by him. The building, which was damaged in a fire and remained abandoned for a while, was restored. The mansion, built in wooden frame construction technique, consists of two parts: haremlik and greetings. Interior walls of the 4-storey mansion with a view of the bay; It is decorated with various landscape paintings and rich hand-drawn works. It is rumored that these decorations were decorated by painters who came for the restoration of Dolmabahçe Palace. The mansion, which is preferred as a shooting location in historical TV series, is also among the places frequented by photography lovers.

TARİHİ EREĞLİ EVLERİ: Karamürsel-Ereğli, a charming seaside settlement, once featured in Yeşilçam movies with its unique houses. Ereğli houses are lined up like pearls parallel to the coast, with their styles reflecting the traditional characteristics of the Ottoman-Turkish house. These masonry and wooden houses, most of which are two-storey, attract the attention of both daily tourists and photography enthusiasts with their historical atmosphere.

YALI EVLERİ: Within the scope of the "History Corridor Project", outstanding examples of Ottoman architecture were restored and brought to light again and opened to visitors as Waterside Houses in Gölcük Değirmendere. With the approval received by the Cultural and Natural Heritage Board, 12 buildings and mosques that were restored to their original form were turned into social living spaces. The restored buildings also host social and cultural activities such as cafes, boutique hotels, art galleries, ceramic workshops and libraries.

Historical Places

ESKİHİSAR KALESİ: Eskihisar Castle was once an important crossing point that kept the south of Izmit Bay under control. Although he does not have an inscription, the Byzantine historian Georgios Akropolites mentioned him for the first time in 1241, and his name was later mentioned in history books due to the Battle of Palekanon. Its architectural features indicate that it was built during the Byzantine period, during the Komnenos Dynasty (1081-1185). The entrance of the castle, located within the borders of Gebze, is also suitable for concert events.

HEREKE KALESİ: The conquest of the castle in 1326 complemented the conquests of the Izmit region and became a part of the Ottoman principality. However, Sultan II. During the reign of Bayezid, a great earthquake occurred. Hereke Castle was completely destroyed in the earthquake. After the disaster, the people of Hereke could not recover for a long time, and the weaving industry for which they were famous went into stasis for a while. Restoration work has been carried out in Hereke Castle, which is thought to have been built in the 640s during the Eastern Roman Empire and was also mentioned in Evliya Çelebi's Travelogue, and was awarded the best project award in recent years.

KONCA LAHDİ: MS. II. The tomb, built in the Roman period in the 16th century, is known as the "Roman Tomb" in the region. The tomb has a form similar to other sarcophagi in the Bithynia region. While there is an inscription on the side of the sarcophagus facing the sea, a male and female figure and a standing maid are depicted on the other side. The inscription of the sarcophagus contains the following statements: "Poidonei, sons of Artemata and Artemidoros, and Tatia, daughter of Apollonios, had this sarcophagus prepared for them while they were alive. Greetings to those passing by and those passing by the sea.”

MEHMET BEY HAMAMI – ORTA HAMAMI: Dating back to the 16th century, the Middle Bath is one of the works built by Mehmet Bey, the founder of the Fevziye Mosque, in order to generate income for the mosque. It is thought that this building, which is a single bath, may have been built by Mimar Sinan during the construction of the Pertev Pasha Complex, due to its similarity in plan and construction technique to the Pertev Pasha Bath, which is a double bath. The bath consists of cold, warm and hot sections.

SÜLEYMAN PAŞA HAMAMI: It was built by Prince Süleyman, who also had the Orhan Gazi Mosque built. Prince Süleyman is the son of Orhan Gazi. Izmit and its surroundings were given as a fief by his father to the prince who participated in the conquest of Iznik in 1330 and Izmit in 1337. Süleyman Pasha Bath is the earliest Ottoman period bath structure in Izmit that has survived to the present day. It is in the form of a double bath with male and female sections. Both parts are planned equally to each other. It consists of a cold section at the front covered with a double high dome, and two private private rooms at the back covered with a low dome, connected to each other by a door. Each compartment has three domes.

GÜLTEPE NEKROPOLÜ: Necropolis means "city of tombs" in ancient Greek. The Ancient Necropolis and the remains of a "sacred building" from the Byzantine Period were found by chance during road works in the hypogeum (catacombs). There are also triangular-shaped terracotta "bone boxes" in the region, called Tile Tombs from the Roman Period, where the bones of cremated and boiled corpses were placed. In the upper parts, Roman Period sarcophagi dating back to the second and third centuries can be found.

ÜÇ TEPELER TÜMÜLÜSÜ: Approximately 3 km from the centre of Izmit, on the old Istanbul road, the village of Kabaoğlu-Üçtepeler is greeted by tumuli, which are thought to be the tombs of the kings who once ruled in these lands. Three of the tumuli, which give the region its name, are inside the village and four are outside the village. Aytepe Tumulus is dated to the Late Hellenistic-Early Roman Period. As a result of the archaeological excavations carried out in the large tumulus belonging to the Roman Period, it was understood that it had been robbed during its period, and no finds from the tomb have survived to the present day.
